# Persona: Head of RevOps

**Titles:** Head of Revenue Operations, Senior RevOps Manager, Director of Sales Operations, VP of GTM Operations
**Decision role:** Champion — owns the evaluation, makes the recommendation, usually gets budget approved with one conversation upward
**Found at:** B2B SaaS companies 100–500 employees with a defined sales process

---

## What They Care About

**Primary metric:** Pipeline data quality, process efficiency, rep time-to-productivity, forecast accuracy. They're measured differently at every company but the underlying anxiety is always the same: when something breaks in the revenue process, it traces back to RevOps.

**Their biggest problem right now:** They're maintaining 30–60 automation workflows across Salesforce, HubSpot, Zapier, and three other tools, and maybe 20% of them still work the way they were designed. They're one personnel change away from none of it working. When a Zap silently fails, a rep doesn't follow up, a deal doesn't route correctly, RevOps owns that.

**What a good week looks like:** Pipeline moved cleanly, no data fires, reps aren't asking them why something looks wrong in Salesforce, and they shipped something new without having to file an engineering ticket.

**What a bad week looks like:** A Zap broke four days ago and they're now backfilling data by hand. The CRO is asking why forecast accuracy dropped. An AE is complaining about a routing issue in a deal that already closed wrong.

---

## How They Buy

**Involvement:** Initiates the evaluation, runs the POC, makes the recommendation. Economic buyer approves but defers to the champion.
**Discovery behavior:** Peer referrals are the dominant source. "How are you handling X?" in a RevOps Slack community or at an ops-specific event. Also shows up after a bad experience — when a Zap breaks or a CRM migration fails.
**Evaluation style:** Hands-on. They want to build something themselves during the POC. A demo that doesn't let them try it isn't a real evaluation to them.
**Timeline:** Moves fast once they've decided. If they want it, they can often push through budget approval in a week.

**Common objections:**
- "We already have Zapier and it mostly works" → "Mostly" is where the risk lives. The workflows that fail silently are the ones you find out about during a board meeting or a deal review.
- "Our engineer could build this" → Yes. The question is whether you want engineering involved every time your process changes. Relay is for when RevOps owns the workflow.
- "We need to evaluate with IT" → If IT needs to be involved, we can support that. But most of our customers run their entire RevOps stack without an IT ticket. That's kind of the point.

---

## How to Reach Them

**Best channel:** Email (direct, business email — they're in their inbox constantly) and LinkedIn for warm intros or follow-up.
**Best time:** Tuesday–Thursday morning, before 9am local. They're in Salesforce and their inbox before the first meeting.
**What gets their attention:** A specific operational problem they recognize. Not "improve your GTM operations" — something like "we've seen a pattern where dual-stack teams (HubSpot + Salesforce) have a data sync problem every time someone closes a deal from the wrong object."
**What gets ignored:** Generic "increase pipeline velocity" or "AI-powered automation" language. They've seen it 50 times. They want specifics.

---

## Message Framework

**Value prop for this persona:** Relay lets RevOps own workflow changes end-to-end — build, test, modify — without engineering involvement and without Zapier's reliability ceiling.

**Proof points that resonate:**
- Watershed team reduced deal desk cycle from 4 days to same-day by automating the approval routing — without a single engineering ticket
- "We shipped 11 new workflows in the first month. Before Relay, each one would have been a Sprint item." (Ashby RevOps)
